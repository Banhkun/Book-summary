Part 2: Find and Keep a Man | Chapters 5-6, 9, 13: Tips for Getting a Man

In this second part of the guide, we’ll examine Harvey’s strategies for finding and keeping a man. In this chapter, we’ll specifically explore his tips for the first stage in any relationship: actually getting a man in the first place .

Figure Out What He Wants

According to Harvey, the first thing to consider when you start to date a man is what he wants from you. Does he want to pursue you in the hopes of starting a relationship—or, is he just interested in sleeping with you? Harvey argues that in the vast majority of cases, it’s the latter scenario. Most men only want to have a fun, physical fling with you, and if you expect more from them—in other words, commitment—you’ll only be disappointed.

(Shortform note: Why might a man shy away from commitment and pursue only casual relationships? According to clinical psychologist Diana Kirschner, men who eschew commitment often do so out of fear . This fear can manifest in various ways, including fear of rejection, fear of not meeting expectations, or fear of revealing their flaws. Kirschner argues that these men may also fear that, if they get into a relationship, the woman will start to control and smother them, acting more like a second mother than a partner.)

Of course, Harvey notes, if you’re only looking for a casual fling or a one-night-stand—which is your prerogative—the fact that so many men only want sex isn’t a problem for you. You can date pretty much any man and be sure that you’ll get what you want. However, Harvey muses, what if you want a serious relationship? How do you spot the few men who want commitment in a sea of men who don’t?

According to Harvey, the key is to tell a man up front that you’re looking for commitment, not just fun. In most cases, if a man chooses to pursue you after you’ve told him this, he’s looking for a serious relationship, too. If he’s just looking for sex, he’ll probably leave you alone. Harvey emphasizes that being up-front in this way will save you a lot of pain—it’ll prevent you from wasting time and energy on a man who only sees you as a temporary fling.

In Harvey’s view, other signs that a man isn’t looking for commitment include:

  • Showing a lack of interest in you as a person
  • Waiting for more than 24 hours to call you following a date
  • Not introducing you to his friends
  • Being late to dates

More Ways to Tell Whether He Wants Sex or Commitment

Relationship experts are divided on the validity of Harvey’s signs that a man isn’t looking for commitment. For example, Cosmopolitan agrees that a man who shows up late without a good excuse or who won’t introduce you to his friends is likely uninterested in a serious relationship. Similarly, dating coach Ronnie Ann Ryan argues that a man who’s not genuinely interested in your life doesn’t want a relationship , because he’s focusing on himself instead. However, waiting more than 24 hours to contact you following a date may be a strategy: Elle explains that some men follow the three-day rule , which states that they must wait three days before contacting you to avoid looking overeager.

But even if Harvey’s tips don’t always apply, there are other ways to tell whether someone wants to pursue a casual or a serious relationship with you.

For instance, if a man makes all of his compliments to you appearance-based, doesn’t take you on dates, and tries to make every conversation he has with you about sex, he probably just wants to sleep with you . Further, if, whenever he comes around to your place, he rushes to the bedroom—and gets frustrated if you tell him you want to do something other than have sex, like have dinner together—it’s likely that sex is all that’s on his mind.

Conversely, couples therapist Alisha Powell notes that if your man frequently reaches out just for the pleasure of talking to you , and, during conversation, opens up about his life experiences, worries, and goals, he’s committed to you.

Act Like You Want a Long-Term Relationship (If You Do)

We’ve discussed how to find out whether a man is interested in commitment—now, let’s explore how, in Harvey’s view, you can signal to men whether or not you’re in the market for a long-term relationship.

According to Harvey, the very first time you meet a man, he’ll begin to analyze how you look, act, and speak to figure out whether you’ve got the potential to be his long-term partner. For this reason, if you are looking for a relationship, it’s important to know how you should and shouldn’t present yourself so you give men the right impression. In the table below, you’ll find Harvey’s recommendations for how to behave—and not behave—if you want a man to see you as relationship material:

Behaviors That Signal You Want a Relationship Behaviors To Avoid If You Want a Relationship
Wearing clothes that are sexy, but modest Wearing revealing clothes
Initiating deep conversations on a range of topics Only making conversation to flirt
Being willing to meet, and be respectful to, a man’s friends and family Refusing to meet a man’s friends and family
Keeping a cool head and being polite in times of conflict Losing your temper and snapping in times of conflict
Making plans for the long-term Making no plans beyond this weekend
Complimenting and appreciating your man for what he does right Constantly complaining about what your man does wrong
Being loyal to your man Keeping your eyes peeled for a “better” man than the one you’ve got
Enjoying a night out from time to time, but not too often Partying to excess

What Other Experts Say: How to Behave When You’re Looking for a Relationship

Relationship experts are divided on whether following the behaviors Harvey recommends are helpful if you’re looking for a relationship.

Strategies they support include dressing modestly and keeping a cool head. By dressing modestly, you put the focus on your personality instead of your looks . By doing so, you’ll weed out anybody who just wants sex; moreover, if you do end up in a relationship, you won’t grow anxious that he only likes you for your looks. Similarly, learning to fight healthily .)—like by remaining polite instead of losing your temper—is a key predictor of relationship success.

Some strategies, however, they believe are effective only with caveats. In Why Men Love Bitches , relationship expert Sherry Argov warns against conversations that are overtly flirty or sexual; however, she recommends that you should still flirt subtly during your conversations about other topics. Similarly, while Argov prefers compliments to complaining, she reminds you to only compliment a man for something once : Praising him excessively for the same thing will make you seem desperate and diminish his interest in you.

In contrast, other experts disagree with some of Harvey’s strategies—not because they’re inherently bad, but because at this stage, you’re not yet in a committed relationship. Notably, they recommend against being loyal to a man you’re not in a committed relationship with; instead, you should date multiple people simultaneously so you don’t get too upset if one of them dumps you. Similarly, while long-term plans might be good for you, you shouldn't make long-term plans with him yet ; if you are, you’re probably moving too fast. And even Harvey himself urges that you only meet your man’s family after you are officially together—a topic we’ll elaborate on later.

Finally, they disagree with one strategy that is inherently problematic: changing your party behavior. If you want to change, that’s fine; but you ultimately want someone with whom you’re compatible —and if you lead a heavy party lifestyle, you probably need someone who can keep up with that reality.

Note that if you don’t want a relationship and instead want only casual romances—which, Harvey emphasizes, is a perfectly valid choice—implementing the behaviors in the right-hand column will quickly get you what you want. According to Harvey, men will line up to be with a woman who makes it clear she’s just out for a good time. (Shortform note: Other ways to signal to a man that you’re only looking for some casual fun include regularly bringing up sex in conversation, frequently initiating physical contact, and making flirty eye contact .)

Have (and Enforce) Standards for Your Man

According to Harvey, another important facet of finding the right man for you is setting and enforcing standards. Standards are non-negotiable expectations that you expect a potential partner to meet—for instance, he must have a good sense of humor, or he must want kids.

Harvey argues that the primary purpose of setting and enforcing standards is to weed out men who aren’t right for you. If you know what your standards are and realize that a potential partner either can’t or won’t meet those standards, you know to break things off.

(Shortform note: While Harvey exclusively discusses why you should set standards for your partner, it may be useful to set standards for yourself , too. In Awaken the Giant Within , life coach and speaker Tony Robbins argues that having a set of rules that you self-enforce —for instance, that you must always treat people with kindness, or that you must work out three times a week—can push you to become your best self. In terms of dating, you may set yourself standards that help you become the best partner you can be. For example, one of your rules might be to treat your partner to one date night per week, regardless of how busy you are at work.)

Figuring Out Your Standards

As Harvey notes, everyone’s standards are different—and that’s fine. We all want different things from relationships, so it follows that our expectations would be unique, too. But how can you figure out what your standards are? According to Harvey, the best method is to figure out the answers to the following questions:

Personality traits: What personality traits do you look for in a man?

Relationship expectations: What will your man need to do to satisfactorily maintain the relationship? (For example, will you expect him to stay at your place every weekend and text you every day?) Will you expect him to be exclusive right off the bat, or can he sleep with other people?

Career: Does a man’s wealth and/or occupation matter to you, or are you happy to date people from all walks of life? Does he have career goals that align with your plans for the future? (For instance, if his goal is to get a job that allows him to travel a lot, are you happy to be with someone who’s away from home frequently?)

Family: Do you want kids? Are you happy to date someone who already has kids? Is it important for your man to get on well with both his and your family?

Religion: If you’re religious, do you only want to date people who share your religion? If you’re not religious, would you date someone who is?

How to Create Standards That Work for You

Other experts caution that your relationship expectations must be realistic ; otherwise, you’ll never find anyone who meets them and thus won’t find the happiness you seek. Moreover, your expectations may actually backfire: For example, expecting your man to be with you constantly will destroy both of your independence and likely result in a breakup.

To ensure your expectations are realistic, therapist Jason Sackett argues that you can have two “levels” of standards : those that are truly essential in a partner, and those that you’d prefer a partner to have but wouldn’t deem non-negotiable. Sackett calls the latter group “wish list items.” If you live in the United States, you can also use this online calculator to determine how many people live in the country who meet your preferences regarding career, family, and religion. Once you know this number, you can determine whether you should adjust your standards—and how changing a requirement into a wish list item might expand your dating pool.

Even if you do have realistic expectations, you may still wish to alter them if you find that you’re still not getting the kind of relationship you want. Studies suggest that the people you tend to date have similar personalities —but if you also keep breaking up with these people, relationship counselor Natasha Silverman recommends that you re-evaluate your relationship patterns . If you regularly date people who are bad for you, you may only be able to find a healthy relationship by dating people with different personality traits.

Communicating Your Standards

Once you’ve figured out what your standards are, it’s time to communicate them to the person you’re interested in dating. How you choose to do this is important. According to Harvey, if you approach a man and demand he adhere to numerous rules if he wants to date you, he’ll be unimpressed and unlikely to agree. Nobody likes being told what to do—especially men, who, Harvey argues, feel emasculated if they feel they’re being “forced” to follow certain rules. Furthermore, men crave agency: They want to feel like they figured out your standards all by themselves.

Therefore, Harvey believes that the best way to communicate your standards is subtly. Weave your standards into natural conversation, and don’t state outright that they’re your standards— just hint that you like or dislike certain behaviors. That way, you’ve given your man the breadcrumbs he needs to figure out your expectations, without dictating them to him. For example, rather than telling your man, “You must send me a ‘good morning’ text every day,” you might say, “Isn’t it nice to have a text from your partner to wake up to every day?”

How to Communicate Your Standards Nonviolently

As we’ve noted, Harvey advises against demanding that your partner adhere to your standards—they’ll probably become annoyed and say no. His view aligns with that of Marshall B. Rosenberg, author of Nonviolent Communication , who argues that demanding things from someone will alienate them and damage your budding connection . According to Rosenberg, demands are alienating because they carry the implicit threat of punishment or upset if the other person doesn’t do as you ask.

So how should you present your standards to your partner, if not through a demand? Here, Rosenberg’s views diverge from Harvey’s. While Harvey suggests you shouldn’t directly ask your partner to follow your standards at all (and should instead merely hint at what they are), Rosenberg argues that you can make direct requests of someone —as long as you do so respectfully and give the person the space to say no if they want to. (If you won’t take no for an answer, you’re back into the harmful territory of demanding things.)

According to Rosenberg, if someone refuses a request you’ve made—in our case, if they push back against your proposed standard—it’s important to try to see the situation from their point of view. Why did they say no? What might make the request more palatable to them? Rosenberg argues that showing empathy in this way will strengthen your relationship with the other person. Furthermore, it’ll keep you open to the option of compromise—in other words, finding a way to amend the request so that it works for everyone.

In terms of relationship standards, finding a compromise can sometimes be impossible (and inadvisable) . For instance, if one of your standards is that you want kids, and your potential partner refuses to meet that standard because they want to stay child-free, there’s no real way you can meet in the middle. Furthermore, it wouldn’t be fair to try to force him to have children. Therefore, it’s better to part ways.

However, in some scenarios, compromise may be more practicable. For example, if your initial standard was that you and your potential partner should talk on the phone three times a week, but he only wants to speak once per week, you might both find that you can accept speaking twice a week.

Don’t Be Too Independent

In today’s society, strong, independent women—women who have a career and money and can look after themselves—are championed. But Harvey argues that if you want to find a man who will stay with you for the long term, you can’t be too independent.

According to Harvey, men need to feel useful, strong, and purposeful. If you do everything for yourself and never let your man help you, you won’t fulfill those needs, and your man will be unhappy.

Furthermore, as we discussed in Part 1, men show their love by looking after the people they care about. If you’re too independent, you won’t give your man chances to look after you, meaning he won’t be able to show his love. Harvey claims that if men can’t show love in the way they want to, they quickly move on.

How to Stop Your Independence From Ruining Your Relationship

How can you maintain some independence but still keep your man happy? Harvey argues that you should allow your man to do things for you, even if you don’t strictly need him to. For example, you might know exactly how to change a tire—but would it really hurt if you let your man do it for you instead? Arguably not—and in the process, you’ll be fulfilling your man’s need to look after you. You’ll be helping him to keep the delusion that he’s in control and is needed.

What Other Relationship Experts Say About Independence

In Why Men Love Bitches , Argov agrees with Harvey that being too independent will harm your relationship, but she argues that you must strike a balance between independence and dependence to have a happy relationship.

Argov contends that a man’s desire for you increases when he thinks you’re just hard enough to get—but not too hard. As such, you must be independent in the sense that you can handle the basic necessities of life; you can’t seem like you wouldn’t survive without your man. However, Argov explains, a man’s desire for you also increases when he feels that you appreciate him for how manly he is—so at the same time, you should provide your man with opportunities to make him feel manly.

How can you make him feel manly? Like Harvey, Argov recommends letting your man do things for you that you don’t actually need him to do. She likens stroking your man’s ego in this way to your man giving you flowers: He doesn’t care about the flowers, but he wants to make you happy; similarly, stroking your man’s ego in this way may not matter to you but will improve his mood and thus your relationship. Argov emphasizes that you should make a point of letting your man do things for you in public to further stroke his ego: For example, let him talk to the hostess at the restaurant.
