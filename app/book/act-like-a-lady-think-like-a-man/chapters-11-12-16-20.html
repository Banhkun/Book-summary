Chapters 11-12, 16, 20: Tips for the Early Stages of a Relationship

So, you’ve found a man, and you’ve both decided to give dating a try—what next? In this section, we’ll discuss Harvey’s tips for how to behave in the early stages of a relationship to ensure its long-term success : Namely, withhold sex for the first three months, introduce him to your children, and meet his family.

Withhold Sex for the First Three Months

If you’re looking for a committed relationship, Harvey recommends that you withhold sex for the first 90 days. Harvey contends that sex is a privilege of being with you, not a right—and you should evaluate him for 90 days to determine whether he’s worthy of that benefit. In this section, we’ll discuss why you should wait for 90 days—and how to get successfully to Day 91.

Why 90 Days?

Why 90 days? Harvey presents two reasons.

First, Harvey notes, 90 days is enough time to weed out any men who are looking solely for sex. But if he’s solely interested in sex, his patience will wane before 90 days is up and he’ll leave. In contrast, a man who will prove his worth to you for 90 days without sex is a man who respects you and as such is someone worth investing your time in .

Second, Harvey notes, 90 days gives you enough time to experience many different scenarios with your man so you can get a good grasp of who he is . Specifically, Harvey explains, you can discover the following things about him:

How he responds when you set boundaries : A man who cares about you respects the word “no” when it comes to sex; a man who doesn’t care disappears. (Shortform note: Other experts add that you should also pay attention to how your man reacts to boundaries in other contexts , like how he responds when you ask him not to text you after a certain time: If he doesn’t respect them, he doesn’t respect you or your feelings.)

How he responds when you need him : A man who cares about you will help you or try to comfort you if you lean on him in small ways; a man who doesn’t care won’t bother. (Shortform note: One relationship writer elaborates on why it matters that your man responds when you need him: If he doesn’t, it indicates that he doesn’t consider you a priority . This is especially true if he repeatedly doesn’t respond when you need him.)

How to Wait for Sex Without Losing Him

In Why Men Love Bitches , Argov also recommends waiting to have sex, but the nuances of her recommendation differ slightly from Harvey’s. Unlike Harvey, Argov doesn’t specify how long to wait, writing that you should wait at least a month but preferably as long as possible. Similarly, although Argov agrees that some men who leave before you’re ready to have sex are those who would have left anyway, she contends that a man may also leave if he takes your lack of interest in sex as a sign that a) you’re not attracted to him, or b) you’re never going to sleep with him.

To prevent him from believing this, Argov recommends that you keep the physical spark alive by being physically affectionate in public during the daytime: Kiss him during your picnic date at the park instead of on the couch at his apartment. This keeps the relationship hot and tells your man you still desire him but doesn’t imply to your man that you want to go further in that moment.

What to Do During Those 90 Days

You now understand why you should wait 90 days—but what should you be doing during those 90 days if you’re not having sex? Harvey presents a month-by-month timeline of what to do during the first three months in order to establish an emotional connection.

The First Month

As Harvey notes, in the beginning of your relationship, you’ll likely grow so infatuated with your man that you’ll drop everything for him. Don’t do this, Harvey warns, implying that by setting and keeping boundaries , you’ll keep a cooler head and be better able to get to know each other better.

To do so, Harvey recommends doing the following:

Stay firm in your standards. If you’ve made it past the first few dates, your man has decided your high price is worth it. Don’t waver during this first month just because you are floating above the clouds.

Work out the little things that could grow into big problems. Small annoyances in the beginning can become dealbreakers later on. Work out these problematic traits now to avoid trouble down the road. His interest in you is piqued, so he’ll be more open to learning what he can do to keep you happy.

Become friends. You may be hot and bothered now, but when the novelty wears off, you’ll want someone you can talk to and laugh with. Form a friendship with your man so you’ll have real things to talk about and can form a real connection. (Shortform note: Simple strategies for improving your friendship with your man include practicing empathy to help make your partner feel understood and enjoying time with your partner by laughing at each other’s jokes.)

Why Sticking to Your Standards Early Matters

In Attached , psychiatrist Amir Levine and psychologist Rachel Heller elaborate on how maintaining firm standards or boundaries early in the relationship might save you trouble further down the line.

They explain that if you’re an “anxious attacher” (someone who constantly seeks assurance from their intimate partners) dating an “avoidant attacher” (someone who avoids intimacy), you may get stuck in a cycle of conflict : Your desire to get closer will drive your avoidant man away. This cycle, they contend, begins with small issues that expand into every corner of life: For example, if your avoidant man doesn’t want to introduce you to his friends at the beginning of the relationship, he may also want to go to their parties alone six months down the line.

To avoid long-term conflicts like this, Levine and Heller recommend expressing your needs directly to the people you’re dating : For example, tell your avoidant man that it’s important for you two to share a social life. By doing so, you’ll be able to weed out the people who are emotionally incapable of fulfilling your intimacy needs—and so avoid the little problems in the relationship that might bloom into large problems that can never be resolved.

The Second Month

To keep the sparks flying in the second month, Harvey recommends the following strategies:

Start showing appreciation for small gestures. A man loves to feel appreciated for his efforts to make you happy. (Shortform note: To express this most effectively, do so using your man’s love language—the behaviors that help your man feel loved . For example, men who feel most loved receiving gifts will appreciate a small token.)

Take your relationship to church. If you or your man are religious or spiritual, now is a good time to explore these deeper levels of each other’s lives. (Shortform note: You can do this even if you are of different faiths. Try praying together , since it’s an aspect of nearly every religion that you can share.)

Bring more adventure to your interactions . Diversify what you do on dates to expand your horizons and prevent things from getting stale. (Shortform note: Some adventurous date ideas include doing a DIY project together or competing against each other—like at a bowling alley.)

Warning Signs

As Harvey notes, Month 2 is the time to start looking for warning signs. If you notice any of these signs, you may want to reconsider whether you enter the final phase of your 90-day plan.

  • Warning Sign 1: He only schedules dates when it’s convenient for him.
  • Warning Sign 2: He doesn’t open up: He won’t introduce you to important people in his life or he’s reticent to talk about his past.
  • Warning Sign 3: He isn’t affectionate in public or spends more time talking to others than to you.

(Shortform note: If your man exhibits these warning signs, he may be an avoidant attacher. In Attached , Levine and Heller explain that avoidant attachers desire some intimacy but feel suffocated when their partner gets too close —so they try to gain some distance by using strategies like the warning signs Harvey lists. If you’re unbothered by these behaviors, you’re likely a secure attacher —someone who is emotionally secure enough to be comfortable with both intimacy and emotional distance in your relationship.)

The Third Month

If you’ve made it to the final month, Harvey notes, you’re likely confident that this man respects you and is worthy of your heart and body. But before you sleep with him, Harvey recommends a few strategies to be sure he is:

Get a second opinion. Include a close friend in your activities to gauge their impression of him. You’ve had your love blinders on for two months, and you may not be seeing everything there is to see about your man. (Shortform note: Experts recommend picking a friend who knows you well but isn't prejudiced against your relationship . Someone who’s struggling to find a partner may not be as open to your new man as you wish.)

Communicate about your past intimacies. You need to know his sexual history before you become part of it. If he scoffs or is cagey about his past, he’s not proving himself worthy of your love. (Shortform note: Knowing your partner’s sexual history will also help you make better sexual health decisions . For example, you may be more inclined to wear a condom with someone who regularly has unprotected sex.)

Be clear about his intentions. You’ve been viewing these past three months as preparation for a lasting relationship, but has he thought of that time in the same way? Know his thoughts—and make sure you’re OK with them—before you invite him into your bed. (Shortform note: One sexologist adds that you should honestly examine your own intentions for the relationship before having sex. For example, you might not really want him as a boyfriend but feel pressured to do so because all your friends are getting married.)

What to Do If the Sex Is Bad

Once you’ve made it to Day 91, Harvey says, you can have sex. However, Harvey warns that this night may not meet your expectations: Two people are not always going to come together perfectly the first time, especially after three months, when the build-up may have created pressure.

If you were disappointed, Harvey recommends that you talk to your man with consideration and affection. By this time, Harvey says, you should have created an environment where honest communication is part of how you relate to each other—and discussing sex is part of that. He’ll be grateful that you’re open about what you want, and you’ll open the doors to better sex in the future.

(Shortform note: Sex expert Caroline Kent recommends two ways to deal when you experience a disappointing first time . First, figure out why the sex was bad. Oftentimes, alcohol is the culprit—drinking before sex helps reduce the nervousness but makes it harder for you to orgasm. Second, when you talk to your partner, watch your language: Instead of using “you”-based statements like “You did,” use “I”-based statements like “I feel” to set the tone for an open conversation.)

Introduce Him to Your Children Early

As Harvey notes, many women with kids withhold them from their man until the relationship becomes serious. However, Harvey contends that you should introduce your new man to your kids in the early stages of the relationship—and that doing so benefits both you and your man.

Introducing your children to your man early benefits you because it allows you to evaluate your man’s potential as a father figure. As Harvey notes, any man who you spend your life with must be good with children—and the best way to determine that is to see how he interacts with them. For example, does he enthusiastically listen to your kid’s boring story or yawn and look at his watch? The right man will be confident, comfortable, and trying to befriend your kids just in case he does become a parental figure someday. He’ll also react appropriately to negative situations: For example, he won’t yell when your toddler knocks over his shaving cream or berate your parenting skills if your teenager dislikes him.

(Shortform note: Similarly, if your man is the one with kids (and not you), you must also determine whether you have a viable future together. While this may involve directly assessing his parenting style by meeting his kids, you can also ask questions to determine whether you see a future with him , like whether he’s able to occasionally prioritize you and what his expectations are about your eventual role in his kids’ lives.)

Despite these benefits, you may still have qualms about introducing a man to your children in the early stage, but Harvey contends that these fears can be quelled. Notably, you may want to protect your kids from getting attached to a man who might one day disappear from their lives. In this case, Harvey urges, remind yourself that you’re not bringing home random strangers—only men in whom you see long-term potential.

What Other Experts Say: How to Introduce Him to Your Children

Like Harvey, single mom blogger Emma Johnson disagrees with the idea that you can only introduce men you’ve been dating a long time to your children. However, Johnson doesn’t argue that you should introduce your man to your kids early; rather she recommends waiting until you’re comfortable .

As Johnson notes, there are several reasons to wait to introduce your man. Notably, Johnson suggests waiting if you think your man will be great with your kids and thus cause you to see him as a father figure for your kids, but you’re not emotionally prepared for such a potentially intense emotional situation.

That said, Johnson adds that introducing your man to your kids relatively early also benefits your kids . She argues that as a parent, part of your job is to model healthy romantic relationships for your kids; if you never show them that you’re dating somebody, you’ll struggle to do that.

If you do introduce him relatively early, she recommends introducing him as the “man I’m dating;” using casual phrasing like this will help prevent your kids from getting too attached. But even if they do get attached, Johnson contends that this is not a big deal: Your kids often get attached to people who disappear from their lives, like their teachers.

Meet His Family

Just as it matters how your man interacts with your family, Harvey notes, it’s also important that you develop a good relationship with his family . Harvey presents several tips for navigating both the introduction and any issues you may face down the line.

(Shortform note: While Harvey focuses on how you can impress your man’s family, remember that your man’s family should also impress you. In The Defining Decade , psychologist Meg Jay explains that if you get married, your man’s family will impact your future happiness, so it’s important that you’re comfortable. If not, she recommends rethinking the relationship : Marrying into a family you don’t fit into might make your life miserable in the future and may also reflect values in your partner that you’ve overlooked.)

First, Harvey notes, you must get on the same page about the status of your relationship . Do this before you meet his relatives; otherwise, you’ll be blindsided if your man refuses to refer to you as his girlfriend. If your man is not clear about his level of commitment to you, don’t meet his family.

(Shortform note: Strategies for making the conversation about your relationship status as stress-free as possible include speaking in person, being as direct as possible, and not demanding an immediate answer; it’s OK if your partner needs a few days to evaluate whether he sees you as his girlfriend.)

Assuming you’re on the same page, it’s time to meet his family—and, as Harvey notes, it’s critical that you make a good first impression . Harvey’s tips for doing so include the following: First, be sincere . Show your man’s family the woman he likes instead of trying to be someone you’re not. Second, dress more modestly than you might if it were just you two. Third, do your research before you go: Ask your man for details about the family members who will be there—and try to remember them when you show up! It will go a long way if you already know that Linda is Aunt Linda who taught him how to ride a bike when he was 10.

(Shortform note: Being sincere, dressing modestly, and knowing details about his family help you make a good impression because they indicate that you’re genuine and respectful. Relationship experts recommend several other ways to genuinely express respect to your boyfriend’s family : Talk to everybody there, offer to help with small things like the dishes, and make compliments about specific things, which will sound more sincere than generic compliments.)

Harvey acknowledges that despite your best efforts, you may still face issues with his family: His mother may dislike you or his brother may have a wandering eye. To navigate these issues, Harvey recommends the following strategies:

First, talk to your man as soon as the problem arises. Since it’s his family, he’ll know how best to approach the issue. (Shortform note: This strategy applies if his family does something wrong—but what if you simply don’t like them? In that case, try waiting to see if your impression of his family improves before you talk to your partner.)

Second, set boundaries . If an issue persists even after your man talks to the culprits, politely tell them that you won’t put up with such behavior. Similarly, Harvey recommends telling your man the maximum amount of time you’re willing to spend with his family: Knowing there’s an end in sight to a family party will ease your stress. (Shortform note: In addition to setting boundaries, it’s critical that you decide in advance what you’ll do if that boundary is crossed . For example, you could agree that your partner will say that you’re tired if his family insists you stay longer at the party, allowing you to make your escape.)

Finally, find quality time for the main players —like by taking his grandmother shopping. It’s easier to connect with someone individually than in large groups—especially if they’re on the fence about you. (Shortform note: If you don’t want to spend time with someone who dislikes you, consider building your relationship with your man’s siblings or cousins instead: They’ll likely be more open to your presence and can influence their older relatives.)
